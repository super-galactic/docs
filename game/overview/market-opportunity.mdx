### A Market Reset, Not a Market Decline

Web3 gaming has moved past its speculative growth phase. Early Play to Earn adoption proved that players were willing to engage with blockchain-based games, but it also exposed the limits of reward-first design. As speculative incentives declined, many first-generation titles lost engagement, not because players rejected ownership, but because gameplay depth and economic sustainability were secondary concerns.

This reset has clarified the real opportunity: games that are fun to play first, with ownership and rewards reinforcing engagement rather than replacing it.

### Design Maturity and Player Expectations

Low-cost chains and scalable infrastructure have existed for several years. What has changed is not access to infrastructure, but how games are designed around it.

Earlier Play to Earn titles often prioritised reward extraction, with gameplay built to serve economic output. As the market matured, players and developers gained clearer insight into what works long term: games where skill, progression, and replayability come first, and economic systems reinforce engagement rather than replace it.

This shift reflects a move away from infrastructure-led experimentation toward design-led execution, where sustainability is achieved through gameplay depth and controlled economic mechanics.

### The Gap Super Galactic Addresses

There is a clear gap between traditional Web2 action games with no ownership and early Web3 games where rewards overshadowed gameplay.

Super Galactic is designed to sit in that gap.

The game is already playable, built around real-time skill-based combat, long-term progression, and scarce game-ready assets. Ownership, rewards, and token mechanics are layered into systems players already understand: upgrades, progression, and optimisation.

This approach targets long-term players rather than short-term extractors.
